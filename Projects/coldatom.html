Cold Atom Laser System (Goodsell Lab, Middlebury College)

I helped build and refine a laser cooling setup for rubidium (Rb), focusing on fiber‑optic integration, alignment, and day‑to‑day stability so the team could spend more time collecting data. The system uses Doppler cooling—tuning laser light slightly below an atomic transition so moving atoms preferentially absorb opposite their motion—reducing their average kinetic energy and enabling observation of quantum behavior.

What We Built (high level)

  • Rb vapor cell with optical access; magnetic coils for trapping/cooling region.
  • Three‑laser configuration (cooling, repump, diagnostic) delivered via fiber to the table.
  • Beam shaping, polarization control, and diagnostics (power taps, irises) for repeatable alignment.

My Contributions

  • Fiber‑optic integration: Designed routing and strain‑relief; selected/installed collimators and patch cords to stabilize pointing and mode quality.
  • In‑house fiber capability: Brought a fusion splicer online and produced custom fibers to match our layouts, cutting lead times.
  • High‑quality splices: Achieved ~90% power retention (typical) across splices, minimizing interconnect loss.
  • Alignment & stability: Standardized beam heights, apertures, and quick‑check procedures for faster daily bring‑up with less drift.
  • Thermal/mechanical details: Managed cable routing, mount selection, and heatsinking to reduce long‑term drift.
  • Documentation: Wrote setup notes and checklists so others could reproduce the alignment process.

Results / My Impact

  • Improved the test setup for observing quantum behavior in laser‑cooled Rb—longer, more stable runs.
  • Established in‑house custom fiber builds → faster reconfiguration and reduced dependence on long lead‑time orders.
  • ~90% splice retention is typical, keeping optical power budgets healthy across the system.
  • Smoother daily startup: less time re‑aligning, more time on experiment.
