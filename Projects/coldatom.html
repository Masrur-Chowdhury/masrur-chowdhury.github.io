Cold Atom Laser System (Goodsell Lab, Middlebury College)

In Prof. Anne Goodsell’s AMO (atomic, molecular, and optical) physics lab, I helped build and refine a three‑laser rubidium cooling system—including fiber‑optic delivery, precision alignment, and thermal management—to establish reliable starting conditions for experiments on highly excited (Rydberg) atoms. The aim: stable laser‑cooled atoms suitable for controlled excitation and field‑interaction studies.

System Overview

The setup centered on a rubidium vapor cell in a vacuum environment, illuminated by three diode lasers (cooling, repump, and auxiliary/diagnostic), overlapped at the trap region. Magnetic field coils provided the necessary spatially varying field to support cooling/trapping, while fiber‑optic routing improved beam pointing stability and mode quality. Power meters and irises aided diagnostics, and thermal/mechanical isolation helped maintain alignment over time.

My Contributions

  • Laser paths & alignment: Co‑aligned three beams to the interaction region using apertures, irises, and viewing cards; tuned overlap and polarization where required.
  • Fiber integration: Implemented fiber‑optic delivery (collimators/patch cords) to reduce pointing drift and simplify beam routing on the table.
  • Thermal/mechanical stability: Managed cable strain relief, heat sinking for diode heads, and mount selection to reduce drift.
  • Diagnostics: Set up power monitors and beam sampling for repeatable alignment/throughput checks.
  • Documentation: Maintained schematics and procedures for repeatable startup and alignment.

Why Laser Cooling?

Laser cooling prepares atoms with low velocity spread, enabling precision control and measurement. For highly excited (Rydberg) states—whose induced dipole moments can be large—cold starting conditions improve signal‑to‑noise, allow finer electric‑field control, and support reproducible state preparation for downstream experiments.

Build & Alignment Highlights

  • Beam quality: Fiber delivery to improve Gaussian profile and pointing stability.
  • Mode matching: Adjusted lenses/collimators to achieve beam sizes appropriate for cooling/repump paths.
  • Magnetic field setup: Verified coil placement and polarity; ensured optical access and cable clearance.
  • Thermal management: Managed diode temperature control and minimized thermal gradients at mounts.
  • Repeatability: Created a quick‑check routine (power, beam height, aperture pass) for daily alignment.

Challenges & Learnings

Achieving stable overlap between multiple beams in a constrained geometry requires tight control of mechanical rigidity, thermal drift, polarization, and mode quality. Fiber routing and a disciplined alignment sequence reduced day‑to‑day variance. The work strengthened my intuition for beam diagnostics, vacuum/optics interplay, and repeatable lab procedures.

Skills Gained

  • Laser alignment & safety; polarization control and beam diagnostics
  • Fiber‑optic routing, collimation, and coupling optimization
  • Opto‑mechanical mount selection, strain relief, and thermal management
  • Basic vacuum practice around atomic vapor cells and optical access
  • Procedure writing, setup reproducibility, and experiment documentation
  • Foundations for Rydberg‑atom excitation and electric‑field interaction studies
